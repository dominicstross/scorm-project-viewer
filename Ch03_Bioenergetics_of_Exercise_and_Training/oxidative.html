Oxidative

Oxidative

The oxidative (aerobic) system is the body's primary energy provider during rest and low-intensity activities. It relies on the utilization of carbohydrates and fats as its primary substrates, with protein playing a minimal role except during prolonged exercise or starvation. Understanding how this system functions is crucial for comprehending energy metabolism and its impact on physical performance. The oxidative system's flexibility in substrate utilization allows the body to adapt to various exercise intensities and durations, ensuring a continuous supply of ATP.

This system's complexity involves multiple stages, including glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain (ETC). These processes work in concert to extract energy from substrates, ultimately producing ATP. The efficiency and control of the oxidative system are vital for sustaining physical activity and maintaining cellular function. Factors such as enzyme activity, substrate availability, and the presence of oxygen play critical roles in regulating this intricate energy pathway. Oxidative

The oxidative system is the main energy source at rest and during low-intensity work, utilizing carbohydrates and fats, with protein contributing little except in extended exercise. At rest, fats provide most ATP, but as exercise intensifies, the body shifts to carbohydrates. In prolonged submaximal activity, fats and some protein become the primary fuels.

Glucose and glycogen oxidation starts with glycolysis, and in the presence of oxygen, pyruvate becomes acetyl-CoA in the mitochondria, entering the Krebs cycle. This cycle produces ATP, NADH, and FADH2, which power the ETC. The ETC uses these molecules to create ATP via oxidative phosphorylation, with oxygen as the final electron acceptor. Glucose yields about 38 ATP, and glycogen yields about 39 ATP.

Fats are broken down into free fatty acids, which undergo beta oxidation in the mitochondria, producing acetyl-CoA and hydrogen protons. This process yields many ATP molecules. Protein can also be used, converting amino acids to glucose or Krebs cycle intermediates, contributing more during long workouts. Nitrogen waste is removed as urea and ammonia.

The Krebs cycle is regulated by isocitrate dehydrogenase, which is stimulated by ADP and inhibited by ATP. NADH and FADH2 availability, and GTP levels, also influence the cycle. The ETC is stimulated by ADP and inhibited by ATP. Figures 3.6, 3.7 and 3.8, and table 3.1 provide further information.

Outline: The Oxidative (Aerobic) Energy System

Overview of the Oxidative System

  • Primary ATP source at rest and during low-intensity activities.
  • Uses carbohydrates and fats as primary substrates.
  • Protein contributes minimally, increasing during prolonged exercise or starvation.
  • Substrate utilization shifts based on exercise intensity.

Substrate Utilization Shifts

  • At rest: ~70% ATP from fats, ~30% from carbohydrates.
  • Increasing exercise intensity: Shift from fats to carbohydrates.
  • High-intensity aerobic exercise: Almost 100% carbohydrates.
  • Prolonged submaximal exercise: Shift back to fats and minimal protein.

Glucose and Glycogen Oxidation - Glycolysis

  • Begins with glycolysis.
  • In the presence of oxygen, pyruvate is converted to acetyl-CoA.
  • Acetyl-CoA enters the mitochondria.

Glucose and Glycogen Oxidation - Krebs Cycle

  • Acetyl-CoA enters the Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle).
  • Produces ATP (via GTP), NADH, and FADH2.
  • NADH and FADH2 transport hydrogen atoms to the ETC.

Glucose and Glycogen Oxidation - Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

  • NADH and FADH2 power the ETC.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation: ATP production from ADP.
  • Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water.
  • NADH yields ~3 ATP, FADH2 yields ~2 ATP.

ATP Yield from Glucose and Glycogen

  • Glucose: ~38 ATP.
  • Glycogen: ~39 ATP.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation accounts for over 90% of ATP synthesis.

Fat Oxidation

  • Triglycerides broken down by hormone-sensitive lipase.
  • Free fatty acids enter muscle fibers and undergo beta oxidation.
  • Beta oxidation produces acetyl-CoA and hydrogen protons.
  • Acetyl-CoA enters the Krebs cycle, hydrogen protons go to the ETC.
  • High ATP yield (e.g., >300 ATP from palmitic acid).

Protein Oxidation

  • Amino acids broken down through various metabolic processes.
  • Gluconeogenesis: Amino acids converted to glucose.
  • Minimal contribution during short-term exercise.
  • Increased contribution during prolonged exercise (3-18%).
  • Branched-chain amino acids are primarily oxidized.
  • Nitrogenous waste: Urea and ammonia.

Control of the Oxidative System - Krebs Cycle

  • Rate-limiting step: Isocitrate to Î±-ketoglutarate.
  • Enzyme: Isocitrate dehydrogenase.
  • Stimulated by ADP, inhibited by ATP.
  • NADH and FADH2 availability affects the rate.
  • GTP accumulation inhibits the initial reaction.

Control of the Oxidative System - ETC

  • ETC inhibited by ATP.
  • ETC stimulated by ADP.

Summary

  • Oxidative system is the primary ATP source at rest and during low-intensity exercise.
  • Carbohydrates and fats are the main substrates.
  • Substrate utilization shifts based on exercise intensity and duration.
  • Glucose/glycogen oxidation involves glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and ETC.
  • Fat oxidation yields high ATP through beta oxidation.
  • Protein oxidation is minimal, but increases during long duration exercise.
  • The system is tightly regulated by enzymes and substrate availability.

1. What are the primary substrates utilized by the oxidative system?

Carbohydrates (glucose and glycogen) and fats.

2. Describe the role of the Krebs cycle in oxidative metabolism.

The Krebs cycle is a series of reactions that oxidizes pyruvate, producing ATP, NADH, and FADH2.

3. Explain the process of oxidative phosphorylation.

Oxidative phosphorylation involves the transfer of electrons through the electron transport chain, utilizing NADH and FADH2 to produce ATP.

4. How does fat oxidation contribute to ATP production?

Fats are broken down into free fatty acids, which undergo beta-oxidation to produce acetyl-CoA, entering the Krebs cycle for further ATP production.

5. What is the role of protein oxidation in energy production during exercise?

Protein oxidation contributes minimally to energy production during most exercise, but can become more significant during prolonged activities.

6. What are the rate-limiting enzymes in the Krebs cycle and the electron transport chain?

Isocitrate dehydrogenase (Krebs cycle) and the enzymes involved in the electron transport chain.

7. How does exercise intensity influence the contribution of different energy systems?

Higher intensities rely more on the phosphagen and glycolytic systems, while lower intensities rely more on the oxidative system.

8. What is the relationship between the rate of ATP production and the capacity of energy systems?

There is an inverse relationship: systems with high ATP production rates (e.g., phosphagen) have lower capacities, while those with high capacities (e.g., oxidative) have lower production rates.

9. How does exercise duration influence energy system contribution?

Longer durations favor the oxidative system due to its greater capacity for ATP production.

10. Can any single energy system provide all the ATP needed during exercise?

No. Multiple energy systems contribute to ATP production during exercise, with their contributions varying based on intensity and duration.
