3.3 Substrate Depletion and Repletion

 

 

 

Introduction

Energy availability is critical for physical performance, and the body relies on various substrates to fuel activities of different intensities and durations. These substrates, including phosphagens like ATP and creatine phosphate (CP) and carbohydrates stored as glycogen, can be selectively depleted during exercise. Understanding the dynamics of substrate depletion and repletion is essential for optimizing performance and recovery. Fatigue experienced during exercise is often linked to the depletion of these key energy sources, particularly phosphagens and glycogen, highlighting their importance in exercise and sport bioenergetics.

The patterns of depletion and repletion for these substrates are influenced by exercise intensity, duration, and type. For instance, high-intensity anaerobic exercise rapidly depletes phosphagens, while prolonged moderate-intensity exercise depletes glycogen. Post-exercise recovery involves the replenishment of these depleted substrates, a process that can be significantly influenced by factors such as nutrition and training adaptations. This knowledge is vital for athletes and fitness enthusiasts seeking to maximize their training outcomes and manage fatigue effectively.

Substrate Depletion and Repletion

During various activities, energy substrates, such as phosphagens (ATP and CP) and glycogen, are depleted, which reduces the energy available from bioenergetic systems and can lead to fatigue. Phosphagen depletion is quicker during high-intensity anaerobic exercise than aerobic exercise. CP can decrease substantially within the first 5-30 seconds of intense exercise and be nearly fully depleted during very intense exercise. ATP concentrations may decrease slightly or up to 50-60% during fatigue. Dynamic muscle actions deplete phosphagens more than isometric actions. ATP levels are sustained by CP depletion, the myokinase reaction, and oxidation of other energy sources. Post-exercise, ATP repletion occurs within 3-5 minutes, and CP repletion within 8 minutes, largely through aerobic metabolism and glycolysis. Aerobic training may increase resting phosphagen levels and decrease their depletion rate at submaximal power outputs. Sprint and resistance training show varied effects on resting phosphagen concentrations.
Glycogen, stored in muscles and the liver, is also depleted during exercise. Training and diet affect resting glycogen levels. Anaerobic and aerobic training can increase resting muscle glycogen with proper nutrition.

Glycogen depletion increases with exercise intensity. Muscle glycogen is more critical during moderate- to high-intensity exercise, while liver glycogen is more important during low-intensity and prolonged exercise. High-intensity intermittent exercise can deplete muscle glycogen significantly. Phosphagens are the primary limiting factor in high resistance, low repetition exercise, while glycogen limits high volume resistance training. Glycogen repletion post-exercise is related to carbohydrate ingestion, with optimal intake being 0.7 to 3.0 g/kg every 2 hours. Muscle glycogen can be fully replenished within 24 hours, but eccentric exercise may require more time.

Summary

Substrate depletion and repletion play a crucial role in exercise bioenergetics. Phosphagens, including ATP and creatine phosphate, are rapidly depleted during high-intensity anaerobic exercise, with repletion occurring within minutes post-exercise through aerobic metabolism and glycolysis. Training adaptations, such as aerobic endurance training, may increase resting phosphagen concentrations and decrease their depletion rate. Glycogen, stored in muscles and the liver, is another critical energy substrate. Its depletion rate is directly related to exercise intensity, with muscle glycogen being more important during moderate- to high-intensity exercise and liver glycogen during low-intensity and prolonged activities. High-intensity intermittent exercise, like resistance training, can significantly deplete muscle glycogen.

Post-exercise glycogen repletion is primarily dependent on carbohydrate ingestion, with optimal intake being 0.7 to 3.0 g/kg every 2 hours. Complete replenishment typically occurs within 24 hours, though eccentric exercise may prolong this process. Understanding these substrate dynamics is essential for optimizing training and recovery, ensuring athletes can perform at their best. The interplay between phosphagen and glycogen depletion and repletion underscores the importance of proper nutrition and training strategies in enhancing performance and mitigating fatigue.

1. What are energy substrates, and how do they relate to exercise performance?

Energy substrates are molecules that provide the starting materials for bioenergetic reactions, such as ATP, CP, glucose, glycogen, free fatty acids, and amino acids. Depletion of key substrates, like phosphagens and glycogen, can limit exercise performance.

2. How does high-intensity exercise affect phosphagen concentrations?

High-intensity exercise rapidly depletes phosphagen stores, particularly CP.

3. What are the primary factors that influence glycogen depletion during exercise?

Exercise intensity and duration.

4. How does the body replenish phosphagen stores after exercise?

Primarily through aerobic metabolism, with some contribution from glycolysis.

5. What is the impact of training on phosphagen concentrations?

Aerobic endurance training may increase resting phosphagen concentrations and decrease their rate of depletion during submaximal exercise. The effects of resistance training on resting phosphagen concentrations are less clear.

6. Where are the primary stores of glycogen in the body?

Muscle and liver.

7. How does exercise intensity affect the rate of muscle glycogenolysis?

Higher exercise intensities result in faster rates of muscle glycogenolysis.

8. What is the role of liver glycogen during exercise?

Liver glycogen is more important during low-intensity exercise and helps maintain blood glucose levels.

9. What factors influence muscle glycogen repletion after exercise?

Carbohydrate intake is the most critical factor, with optimal replenishment achieved with 0.7 to 3.0 g of carbohydrate per kilogram of body weight every 2 hours.

10. How does resistance training affect muscle glycogen stores?

High-intensity resistance training can significantly deplete muscle glycogen, particularly with high volumes of work.
